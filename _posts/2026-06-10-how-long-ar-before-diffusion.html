Question: how long should we train an autoregressive language model before switching it to diffusion?

This is the first blog in what I hope becomes a small series about my recent journey into deep learning. I only started seriously learning deep learning a few months ago. This semester I took Advanced NLP with Sewon Min and Seminar in Deep Learning Theory with Jason Lee. Three topics kept pulling at my attention: diffusion, μP, and Muon. They felt like three different doors into the same larger question I keep circling around: how do we make pretraining more predictable, more principled, and less like folklore?

This post is about the first door: diffusion language models. More specifically, it is about my term project for Sewon's NLP class, where I studied a simple curriculum for pretraining block-diffusion language models.

The question I wanted to test: most open-source diffusion language models are converted from strong autoregressive checkpoints. If we really believed the final model should be a diffusion model, would we still spend almost all of pretraining on the AR objective?

My answer, at the scale I could afford to test, was: probably not. In my sweep, spending about 30% of the training budget on AR and then switching to small-block diffusion worked better than both training diffusion from scratch and doing an AR-heavy conversion.

Why I care about this

I am very interested in pretraining science. This is a slightly dangerous thing to be interested in as an independent researcher, because the cleanest version of many questions seems to require enormous compute. It is easy to look at frontier-scale training and feel like the only honest answer is "wait until you have a datacenter."

But I think there is another way to make progress: turn a huge-scale question into a small testable hypothesis. The result may not settle the frontier-scale question, but it can still tell you whether an idea has the right shape. That was the spirit of this project. I did not try to train a giant diffusion LM. I tried to isolate one knob that current recipes mostly leave implicit: the fraction of compute spent on AR before switching to diffusion.

First I needed a model I trusted

Before asking curriculum questions, I needed to learn how to train a modern-ish transformer at all. I started from a LLaMA-style 70M transformer, then added a collection of architecture and optimizer interventions that I had seen in recent work: QK norm, per-head attention gates, depth-scaled layernorm, value residuals, ReGLU, RMSNorm, and interleaved MoE layers. The optimizer was NorMuon plus AdamW, with Muon handling matrix parameters and AdamW handling embeddings and scalar parameters.

One thing I wanted to check was whether tricks studied mostly in autoregressive models still help once the objective changes. My short version from the class presentation was:

  • Most architecture papers study AR models.
  • I wanted to know whether diffusion models benefit from the same architecture.
  • My finding: probably yes.

On the AR objective, the combined recipe improved best validation loss by about 3.5% over my plain transformer baseline. More importantly for this project, these interventions gave me a stronger and more stable base model for the diffusion experiments. I also got a lot of practical experience here: training MoE models, watching router health, making architecture changes without breaking the training loop, and learning how the optimizer choices show up in loss curves.

This part was not the headline result, but it mattered. A curriculum experiment is only meaningful if the underlying model is not falling apart for unrelated reasons.

The curriculum knob

Block diffusion gives a nice way to think about the problem. AR is like block size 1: predict left to right. Full masked diffusion is like block size equal to the sequence length: denoise a whole sequence. BD3 sits in between: decode blocks left to right, but do diffusion-style denoising inside each block.

So I used a two-stage curriculum:

  1. Train autoregressively for a fraction pAR of the budget.
  2. Switch to BD3 with block size b for the rest.

Current open-source large dLM recipes often live near the high-AR corner: start from a very strong AR base, then do a comparatively short diffusion adaptation. That is a very practical thing to do if the AR checkpoint already exists. But if we are training from scratch and we actually want the final model to be diffusion, it is not obvious that this should be the default.

Half-jokingly, my motivating question became:

If someone wanted a future GPT-scale model to be a diffusion model, should they really spend almost all of the compute training it as an autoregressive model first?

The answer I found

I swept the AR fraction at 70M MoE scale on ClimbMix, with sequence length 512 and a fixed optimizer-step budget. The cleanest result was that pAR = 0.30 was the best switch point for the small and medium BD3 block sizes I tested.

The result that surprised me most was not just that 30% AR was good. It was that the best AR fraction looked approximately independent of the post-switch block size, at least for b ≤ 64. Naively I expected the best switch point to depend strongly on block size. A tiny block is almost AR-like, so maybe it should want more AR pretraining. A larger block is less AR-like, so maybe it should want less. Instead, the optimum stayed around 30%.

My current interpretation is that the useful AR-transfer part saturates early. AR quickly learns things diffusion can also use: token statistics, syntax, semantics, maybe some broadly useful representations. After that, additional AR training increasingly specializes the model for left-to-right prediction. Those later AR-specific circuits may not help diffusion much, even if the diffusion block size changes.

The exception was very large block size. At b = 256, AR initialization failed badly and training BD3 from scratch won. That also makes sense: once the diffusion task is far enough from AR, the warm start stops being a warm start.

Post-switch BD3 block size Best recipe in my sweep Short read
b = 4 30% AR → BD3 AR warm start helps.
b = 64 30% AR → BD3 The same switch point still helps.
b = 256 Scratch BD3 The task is too far from AR.

The failure case was more interesting than I expected

Diffusion language models are often motivated by data efficiency: when unique training data is limited, diffusion objectives may keep improving after AR starts to overfit. So I also tried the curriculum in a data-constrained, multi-epoch setting.

The naive 30% AR recipe failed there. At first this was disappointing, but the failure was also useful. The AR phase overfit early, and in the MoE model the router became unhealthy around the handoff. The BD3 continuation inherited this damage and never caught the scratch BD3 baseline.

The fix was phase-specific regularization. I added weight decay during the AR phase, with no router weight decay and no BD3-phase weight decay. This kept the AR phase from overfitting as hard and preserved router health. The tuned curriculum got back to within about 0.01-0.02 nats of scratch BD3 at the data sizes I tested.

This was a good lesson for me. The best AR validation loss was not the thing that predicted the best BD3 continuation. In one run, the tuned AR phase actually had worse AR validation loss than the no-WD AR phase, but led to a much better diffusion model. The representation you want to transfer is not always the same thing as the source objective's best checkpoint.

What I take away

My current recipe: when pretraining a small-block diffusion language model from scratch, try about 30% AR first, then switch to BD3. If the data is multi-epoch/data-constrained, regularize the AR phase carefully and watch the MoE router.

I do not want to oversell this. This is a 70M-scale experiment with sequence length 512, a fixed optimizer-step budget, and validation loss as the main metric. The exact number 30% is not a law of nature. A wall-clock-fixed comparison may prefer a larger AR fraction, because AR steps are cheaper than BD3 steps in my implementation. Larger models, longer context, different tokenizers, and dense rather than MoE architectures could move the optimum.

Still, I think the result is suggestive. It says that "convert a mostly finished AR model to diffusion" is not the only natural default. If the final objective is diffusion, the useful part of AR may arrive much earlier than current adaptation recipes imply.

More personally, this project made me more excited about pretraining science. I like the combination of theory-shaped questions and very concrete engineering details: optimizer state, router entropy, weight decay groups, loss curves that suddenly tell you something is wrong. I also like the discipline of turning a question that sounds too large for my resources into one I can actually test.

I am still very new to this area, which is part of the fun. A few months ago, I did not know how to train these models. In this project I learned to train MoE transformers, make architecture improvements, use Muon, pick up diffusion objectives, and debug a curriculum that failed for a non-obvious reason. That is exactly the kind of learning loop I want more of.

Next, I want to understand how this interacts with μP and better scaling methodology. If the point is to make pretraining decisions predictable before spending the full budget, then scaling rules are not a side topic. They are the game.
